# Rust to Dust Setting

Rust to Dust is about cultural absorption by a more powerful empire.

The aliens control a galaxy-spanning network. They do not need to defeat
humanity through ordinary conquest. They set up shop, connect Earth to imperial
media, prestige systems, and technology, and make alien culture too beautiful to
ignore.

Their market is overwhelming because of scale, not because they are uniquely
hypercapitalist. Commerce carries the empire, but culture does the conquering.
Their music, art, stories, fashion, rhetoric, ceremony, and disposable
entertainment are all masterworks by human standards. The terror is that the
appeal is real.

The result is not instant occupation. It is generational replacement.

Human children grow up fluent in alien memes and prestige codes. Alien aesthetic
norms enter daily life. Human cultural inheritance does not vanish in one
dramatic fire. It becomes provincial, crude, unfashionable, and then optional.
The old world drains away because the new one is so hard to refuse.

## Core Tension

The setting should make dominant-culture audiences feel the asymmetry they often
export elsewhere: the humiliation of watching their heritage become local color
inside somebody else's civilization.

## Empire Pattern

- The empire frames absorption as access, uplift, taste, and opportunity.
- Military conquest is unnecessary when cultural gravity can do the work.
- Human resistance can be dangerous locally and still become admirable imperial
  performance.
- Human passion is valuable because humans understand beauty and keep reaching
  for it, however crude their work may seem by imperial standards.
- Humanity is unusually compatible with alien values compared with most
  conquered species.
- Most conquered species can be administered, entertained, or improved. Humans
  can answer. That makes them fascinating.
- The empire loves that humans refuse to be conquered. It respects the attempt.
- The empire does not need humans to love it. It needs human life to route
  through imperial meanings.

## Open Naming

The aliens and their language are not named yet. Names should preferably emerge
from the first language project's ontology and phonetics rather than from
English-facing branding.
