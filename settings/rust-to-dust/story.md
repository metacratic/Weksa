# Story

The single-player story follows an ex-military unemployed father made obsolete
by alien technology.

## Opening

The story opens with him catching up with old war buddies in a pub. He complains
that he does not understand his son anymore.

At first, the scene plays like a familiar "kids nowadays" complaint. The twist
is that the generational fracture is not ordinary fashion churn. It is cultural
contamination from a vastly more powerful empire.

A Rust to Dust battle royale broadcast plays on a TV.

The father bitterly complains about the aliens and about humanity giving up on
fighting them. Another customer argues that life has never been better. The
argument resolves, and one of the old war buddies points to the TV and says that
the real fight is not happening there.

He knows a guy.

## Resistance Cell

That contact leads to a global resistance cell. The cell steals important human
cultural artifacts before they can be absorbed into imperial prestige systems,
removed from human continuity, or reframed until their original meaning becomes
secondary.

The work involves a lot of fighting. The father gets into it and becomes good at
it.

## Celebrity Trap

The father becomes a minor celebrity on the alien internet.

He is invited to speak on a panel show. He rants at the audience and hosts,
rejecting their imperialism, values, and culture.

They love it.

His rejection becomes an admired performance. He becomes a meme. That is how his
son finds out.

## Structural Irony

The father thinks the fight is about refusing alien culture. The empire turns
his refusal into proof that humanity has passion and aesthetic potential. His son
encounters him not as a rebel outside the empire, but as someone the empire
understands better than he understands it.
